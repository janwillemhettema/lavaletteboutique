Visiting the Pont du Gard

Two thousand years old, 49 metres high, and still the most impressive single thing in the south of France. A practical guide from people who live fifteen minutes away and have visited it in every season, at every hour.

By the hosts of La Vallette Boutique, Sagriès, Gard

What it is

Two thousand years of engineering

The Pont du Gard is a Roman aqueduct bridge built in the first century AD to carry water from a spring near Uzès to the Roman city of Nîmes, fifty kilometres away. It is the highest surviving Roman aqueduct bridge in the world, and one of the best-preserved monuments from antiquity in Europe. UNESCO listed it as a World Heritage Site in 1985.

What the photographs don't convey is the scale. The three-tiered arcade rises 49 metres above the Gardon river. The longest aqueduct blocks in the upper tier weigh six tonnes each and were positioned without mortar, using only precisely cut stone and gravity. The construction precision is such that the entire 50-kilometre waterway dropped just 17 metres from source to city, a gradient of 0.034 percent maintained across a landscape that includes ravines, hills and this river crossing.

Standing underneath it for the first time, the scale registers before the history does. Then both arrive together.

"Go early in the morning, before the coaches arrive. At seven in June, the light comes from the east across the Gardon and the stone is still cool. You have it almost entirely to yourself."
La Vallette Boutique, Sagriès, 15 min away
When to go

Timing your visit

The Pont du Gard receives over a million visitors a year. The difference between a transcendent experience and a crowded one is almost entirely about timing.

Best, Early morning

7h – 9h30

The site opens at 9h00 in high season (earlier for the riverside). Arriving at opening means the monument before the coaches. The morning light hits the stone from the east. In June and July this is genuinely beautiful. In practical terms: leave La Vallette at 8h30, park in the Rive Gauche car park, walk to the bridge. You will have it to yourself for the first thirty minutes.

Good, Late afternoon

17h – close

The tour groups leave by mid-afternoon. From 17h onwards the crowds thin significantly. The light in summer is warm and low from the west. If you are staying nearby, which you are, at La Vallette, this is also the easiest time to visit spontaneously, after a day elsewhere in the region.

Avoid, Midday in summer

11h – 15h

July and August, 11h to 15h: the car parks are full, the paths around the monument are crowded, and the heat is intense. The experience is still worthwhile, nothing diminishes the scale of the structure, but it is a different visit. If you arrive at this time, go directly to the river and swim first. The crowds on the bridge are worse than the crowds at the water.

Practical information

Tickets, access and logistics

Entry & tickets
Book online
The standard ticket covers access to the monument, the museum (Musée de la Romanité du Pont du Gard), the Ludo educational space, and the riverside. In high season (July–August), booking online in advance is strongly recommended, the car parks fill and ticket queues form. Off-season, walk-in is straightforward. Check the official site for current prices; they vary by season and age. pontdugard.fr, buy in advance for July and August
Parking
There are two car parks: Rive Gauche (left bank) and Rive Droite (right bank). Both are paid. Rive Gauche gives the more direct route to the bridge and is closer to the museum. Rive Droite is quieter and gives access to a different angle on the monument, with a slightly longer walk. From La Vallette, follow signs for Vers-Pont-du-Gard, the drive is 15 minutes on clear roads. Parking fills from around 10h in July and August. Before 9h you will find space easily.
The museum
The permanent exhibition on Roman hydraulic engineering is better than it sounds. Well-designed, multilingual, and genuinely illuminating on the question of how the aqueduct was built and why. Allow 45 minutes to an hour. The scale models of the complete 50-kilometre waterway are worth seeing even if you have no particular interest in Roman engineering.
Walking on top
Upper tier access
Guided tours in high season allow access to the top of the aqueduct, walking along the channel through which the water ran. The view from 49 metres over the Gardon gorge is extraordinary. Numbers are limited; book this as part of your ticket or on arrival. Check current availability at the ticket office.
Opening hours
The site is open year-round, with extended hours in summer. Generally 9h00 to 20h00 in high season, shorter hours in winter. The Musée de la Romanité closes earlier than the outdoor areas. The light show, «Figuration Libre», runs from late May through October, starting at nightfall. Check pontdugard.fr for current hours as they change seasonally.
Swimming

The Gardon river below the bridge

The river Gardon runs directly under the Pont du Gard and is one of the best swimming spots in the region. The water is clear, relatively shallow in summer, and cold enough to matter on a 35-degree afternoon. Swimming is permitted in designated areas on both banks.

The left bank (Rive Gauche) has a long beach area with sand and gravel, good for families and easy entry into the water. The right bank (Rive Droite) has deeper pools and larger flat rocks, better for those who want to swim seriously or sit at the water's edge with the bridge immediately overhead. The view looking up from the water at the three tiers of arches is one of the more unusual perspectives available to a tourist in Europe.

On a hot day in July, the river is as much the reason to visit as the monument. Bring something to lie on, water and food. The snack bar near the car park is fine for a cold drink but nothing more. If you are staying at La Vallette, we can prepare a hamper for the day, ask us when you book.

The light show

Figuration Libre, summer evenings

From late May through 1 November, the Pont du Gard is illuminated each night after dark with a large-scale projection show, «Figuration Libre», created with the Musée d'Art Moderne de Paris. The images run across the full face of the bridge and change each year.

The practical experience: park at Rive Gauche after 19h (parking is often free or reduced in the evening), walk to the riverbank and find a spot. The show begins at nightfall, around 21h30 in June, earlier in September. Bring something to sit on. Take a cardigan; the Gardon gorge is cooler after dark than the surrounding plateau.

This is one of the genuinely good free cultural events of the summer in the south of France. From La Vallette, it is a fifteen-minute drive each way and costs nothing beyond the fuel.

How to make a full day

Combining the Pont du Gard with the region

A

Pont du Gard + Uzès

Morning at the Pont du Gard (arrive at opening). Swim until noon. Drive to Uzès for lunch on the Place aux Herbes. Afternoon in the old town, the Duché, the cathedral, the backstreet markets. Fifteen minutes between the two.

B

Pont du Gard + Gorges

Take the road north from the bridge into the Gorges du Gardon. The canyon landscape above the river is extraordinary, mostly unvisited and accessible on foot or by kayak from Collias. A full day for those who want to walk or paddle.

C

Pont du Gard + Nîmes

Thirty minutes separates the bridge from the Roman city of Nîmes, the best-preserved Roman arena in the world, the Maison Carrée temple, the Jardins de la Fontaine. Two Roman monuments in one day, both genuinely worth the time.

D

Evening light show + dinner

Drive to the bridge at 19h. Walk to the Gardon, find a spot on the bank. Watch the show from 21h30. Return to La Vallette and eat on the terrace. A complete evening that costs almost nothing and uses fifteen minutes of driving in each direction.
