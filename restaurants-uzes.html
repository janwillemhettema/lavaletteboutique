Dining guide · Uzès & the Gard

The best restaurants in Uzès

Uzès punches above its weight. For a medieval town of twelve thousand people, the quality of its tables, its markets and its wider food culture is genuinely remarkable. This is our guide, kept current by the people who live five minutes away.

Updated by the hosts of La Vallette Boutique — Sagriès, Gard

The scene

Why Uzès eats well

The town sits at the intersection of three things that matter: proximity to the coast without the tourist inflation, access to some of the best produce in the Languedoc, and a resident population that takes food seriously. The Place aux Herbes market culture has shaped local expectations for generations. What you find here is not the performance of southern French food, but the real thing.

Restaurateurs in Uzès are working with olive oil from the garrigue, truffles from the surrounding woodland, lamb from the Cévennes, fish from the Mediterranean coast forty minutes south. The terroir is genuine. The cooking, at its best, knows how to stay out of its way.

What follows is a selection we return to — our own shortlist, updated each season. It covers the full range: from a table worth a ninety-minute drive to a wine bar where you can eat standing, happily, for twelve euros.

Fine dining

The serious tables

For a meal that constitutes an event in itself. Book ahead — these are small rooms and the demand is real.

La Table d'Uzès
Michelin ★ Gastronomic Book ahead

The single serious gastronomic address in Uzès. Chef Christophe Ducros holds one Michelin star and uses it quietly — the cooking is precise and seasonal without announcing itself. The menu changes with what arrives from local producers, which is the right approach in this part of the Gard.

The room is handsome without being stiff. Service reads the table correctly. This is the place to come for a birthday, an anniversary, or simply an evening that deserves more than a bistro.

Book at least a week ahead in season. July and August require more notice. Closed certain days — confirm before you go.
Hostellerie Provençale
Classic French Hotel restaurant Terrace

The kitchen here operates in the classic register — not fashionable, not especially modern, but executed with genuine skill and a sourcing ethic that keeps it interesting. The terrace in summer is one of the better places to spend a long evening in Uzès. Portions are generous in the manner of an older France.

Good for groups, good for an extended family lunch on a Sunday. The wine list leans heavily on the Languedoc and Rhône, both reasonable choices for the location.

"The Place aux Herbes on a Saturday morning is one of the great slow pleasures of the south. Arrive before ten, buy something you don't need, drink coffee standing up."

Casual dining

Everyday good

The places you come back to mid-week, or for lunch after the market. Honest cooking, fair prices, no ceremony required.

Le Défi
Bistro Terrace Lunch formula

One of the consistently better bistros on the Place aux Herbes. The kitchen takes its ingredients seriously, which is not guaranteed on a tourist square. The lunch formula is good value and changes with the market — a reliable indicator that someone is paying attention.

The terrace fills quickly after twelve. Arriving at opening or booking is the sensible approach. In the evening the pace slows and the crowd shifts to something more local.

Le Méditerranée
Seafood Simple Market-driven

The coast is forty minutes south, which puts the seafood here in a different category to what you'd find in Paris. The menu is short and honest: grilled fish, shellfish, a well-made bouillabaisse on certain days. Not the place for elaborate presentation — the place for fish cooked by people who grew up near it.

Smaller room than it looks from outside. Worth booking for dinner.
Pizza et cetera
Casual Wood-fired Good for families

There are evenings when the only thing that makes sense is good pizza and an easy glass of something. This is the address for those evenings. Wood-fired, properly made, not pretentious about it. The terrace at the back is quiet by Uzès standards. Reliable in a way that matters after a long day at the Pont du Gard.

Hidden gems

What the locals actually go to

These are the addresses that don't appear in the first results page, and are better for it. We share them with guests who ask.

Le Portalet
Our favourite Wine bar Small plates

The kind of wine bar that Uzès didn't obviously have a decade ago and now anchors a certain kind of evening. The list is well-chosen — small producers, natural-leaning without being dogmatic — and the plates that come with it are exactly the right size and quality.

Go for aperitivo that doesn't end, or as a long preamble to dinner somewhere else. The room is small, the crowd is local and knowledgeable. This is where you understand that Uzès has grown up quietly as a food town.

No reservations for the bar. Arrive at seven and claim a corner. It fills.
La Volti
Backstreet Seasonal menu Intimate

Off the main square, down an unmarked lane, behind a door that doesn't advertise. The menu is short, changes weekly, and reflects what was good at the market that Saturday. There are eight tables. This is exactly as much as it needs to be.

The cooking here is personal rather than professional in the conventional sense: someone decided what they wanted to cook that week and has cooked it. The results are usually better than more elaborate places with longer menus.

Closed Monday and Tuesday. Dinner only Wednesday to Sunday. Essential to book.
Marché d'Uzès
Market Wednesday & Saturday 7h30 – 13h00

The twice-weekly market on the Place aux Herbes is not a restaurant, but it is where the best eating in Uzès actually happens. The Saturday edition draws the region's best producers: truffle dealers from the woodland north of town, cheesemakers from the Cévennes, a fromager affineur who travels from Montpellier, olive oil pressed from groves in the garrigue, honey, charcuterie, seasonal vegetables that haven't seen a supermarket.

Buy bread from the boulanger at the entrance, olives from the stand near the fountain, a wedge of aged tomme and a slice of terrine. Sit on the steps by the Cathédrale Saint-Théodorit and eat. This costs almost nothing and is one of the finer breakfasts in southern France.

Saturday is the larger market. Arrive before 10h for the full selection. Wednesday is smaller but less crowded and easier to navigate.
Le Tracteur, Vers-Pont-du-Gard
15 min drive Farm-to-table Worth the trip

Fifteen minutes from La Vallette, near the Pont du Gard: a restaurant that sources almost everything within a few kilometres and changes the menu when the supply changes. The kind of place that makes the word seasonal mean something again rather than function as menu decoration.

The setting is rural and deliberate — no concessions to tourism, even this close to one of the most visited monuments in France. Combine it with a morning at the Pont du Gard and you have a complete day without trying.

Practical notes

Before you go

01

Hours are French

Lunch runs noon to 14h00, dinner from 19h30 or 20h00. Arriving outside those windows means the kitchen is closed, even if the room is open. In July and August some restaurants extend service — but don't assume it.

02

Closing days vary

Restaurants in Uzès close on different days, and not always the days you'd expect. Calling ahead — or checking their current Instagram — saves the walk across town to a locked door. Especially true outside peak season.

03

Book the serious places

La Table d'Uzès and La Volti require booking. At the bistros on the Place aux Herbes, arriving at opening (noon or 19h30) usually secures a table without a reservation. Weekends in July and August are the exception.

04

The market ends at 13h

Saturday market runs 7h30 to 13h00, at which point it disappears entirely. Build your Saturday morning around this. The best bread, the best cheese and the best produce are often gone by 11h — which is reason enough to arrive early.

05

Wine at the source

The Duché d'Uzès AOC is a young appellation producing interesting wines. Several estates a short drive from La Vallette accept visitors for tasting, often without appointment outside harvest season. Ask us and we'll point you to the ones currently doing good work.

By season

What changes through the year

The food calendar around Uzès is genuinely seasonal in ways that affect what you eat and where. These are the moments worth knowing.

Spring
Apr – Jun
Asparagus from the Gard plain, the first strawberries from Carpentras, spring lamb from the Cévennes. The market is at its most varied and least crowded. The outdoor terrace season begins properly in May. The finest time to eat in Uzès.
Summer
Jul – Aug
Tomatoes, courgette flowers, melons from Cavaillon, peaches that actually taste of something. The restaurants are full, the markets noisy. Book everything. The evening eating culture — long meals that begin at nine and end at midnight — is at its best in July.
Autumn
Sep – Nov
Truffle season begins in November and runs through winter. The cep season arrives in September in good years. The grape harvest brings a different energy to the restaurants — local wines on tables, vignerons at the bar. The quietest and, for food, one of the richest months to visit.
Winter
Dec – Mar
The truffle market moves to the town of Uzès on certain Saturdays in January and February — the most specialised agricultural market in the region, worth the journey. The restaurants that remain open through winter are usually the ones run by people who live here year-round and cook what they want to cook.
